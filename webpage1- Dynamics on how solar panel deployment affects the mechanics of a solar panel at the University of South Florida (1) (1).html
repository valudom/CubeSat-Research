Abstract

For the past couple of years, CubeSat developments have made its leaps and bounds. The construction of these satellites has helped students and the rest of academia in the development and testing of different types of research. Due to the massive number of possible projects when it comes to CubeSat research, one must settle down and decide what information they want to know; pick a specific component to work on and study at a time. This paper concentrates on the CubeSat solar panels and how it affects the dynamics of a system. In this paper, we explore what happens to the CubeSat and its elements in a low orbit system. We will also explore what happens when an (usually from a jet) impulse is applied. Using this information, we can build upon already known information and other students can use the results to figure out other ways to advance/rectify CubeSat technology.

 

Introduction

CubeSats are a class of nanosatellites utilized for research. CubeSats can be recognized by their size which has been standardized to the whopping of . They can be 1U, 2U, 3U, or 6U in size. 1U being the standard size of 10 by 10 by 10 cm all round. CubeSats usually utilize a P-POD for deployment. CubeSats have an inadequate amount of wall space for of walls for large solar cell assembly due to the presence of other parts like the camera lens, access port, sensors, antenna etc. Hence to increase solar panel surface area, hinges must be utilized to create a foldable/extendable array.

Methods

To conduct the tests that we did in Ansys, the first step was creating an accurate model whilst verifying and justifying the material we used to create it. There were 7 distinct portions that made up the model:

·      The base

o   base sheet made from aluminum as a way to efficiently conduct power.

·      The Silicon cell-

o   Solar cells made from silicon. They are usually mono- or polycrystalline structure and are the photovoltaic element of the solar panel. In this case we use anisotropic silicon which is utilized in monocrystalline configurations.

·      The grid lines

o   All extrusions encompassed in this including the faces and linear patterns are made of silver. In some cases, chromium or aluminum can be feasible substitutions.

·      The glass cover

o   Glass cover usually utilizes tempered glass.

o   ANSYS unfortunately does not have tempered glass in its rolodex so viable alternatives must be employed.

o   For the purposes of this experiment, the material was replaced with plexiglass. This would still ensure the reduction of internal humidity and protect the base structures.

o   Most of the plastic used in solar panels are used for connecting components including but not limited to thrust washers, electric insulators, pipes, valves, and others.

·      Hinge

o   An arbitrary hinge was created for observation purposes only and would not be the same type of hinge used in real life processes.

o   ANSYS material used was plastic

Analyses:

Static Structural:

Due to license constraints analysis was only conducted on one solar panel connected to the hinge as a fixed support. The gist of the error message was just that the model had too many bodies to solve on the current system.

When conducting static structural analysis, materials must be assigned to all bodies in your model. This process is very taxing but if you label each part prior to uploading into ANSYS mechanical, you can make use of the search bar.

After this is done, any joints or spring connections you would like to include in your analysis can be input. The mesh is a very important part of the process. If the mesh fails repeatedly, the mesh size should be adjusted and if that does not work, changing the roughness is another option.

After mesh is created, parameters for your intended process can be input. As shown in the photo below, the hinge was used as the fixed support and to simulate deployment of the panel, the driving force also comes from the hinge which was programed as a revolute pin.

Modal Analysis:

Before you can conduct a vibration analysis, modal analysis MUST be done. This makes sense because it will typically take excitation signals like impulse to provide us with the frequency information we need.

“The resulting transfer function [from the analysis] will show one or more resonances, whose characteristic mass, frequency and damping ratio can be estimated from the measurements.”(1)

For the purpose of this analysis, I increased the number of modes to find from the default 6 to 8 to get more data however the number of modes can be changed based on the researcher’s choice. This enabled me to find the total deformation at 8 different modes(frequencies) using the pre-stress conditions post static analysis.

Vibration & Harmonic Response Analysis:

To conduct the vibration analysis, make sure ANSYS completes solving the modal analysis so you can use those results to get what the modal environment will be. To attain the vibrational displacement, square the (max) total deformation and divide that by frequency. Input the values you get into the PSD Displacement table and solve for directional deformation along all 3 axes. This will show the responses caused by the initial forces applied to the model.

 

 

 

 

Similar to the vibration analysis we conducted, a harmonic response is used to calculate instead of displacement response but rather an impulse/force response.

What we would have done/ Things to note:

For ANSYS to work accurately, material information must be input. Having the accurate material selections can be the difference between the simulation’s real-world precision and error.

Failures:

A couple of common place errors to watch out for when conducting system analysis. Possible reasons for why ANSYS Simulations kept failing.

·      Mesh constantly failed when element size was too big

·      proceeded to run continuously if mesh was too small.

·      Use of certain materials causes issues in creating a mesh

·      The coarseness of a mesh should also be considered. If a mesh is too course, the figures attained will be a little off but will return an approximate figure which we used in this case.

 

·      Mass calculations are returning values in tons even though system shows that mode is in kilograms.

When doing an analysis of a CubeSat or component of a Cube Sat in a low orbit space environment, there are certain things to consider like disturbances one might come across while in space. Some of the disturbances one must consider are:

·      Space weather- Space weather is not as chaotic as what we have here, but it still does occur and one of the things, we must watch out for is radiation. This can damage electrical components of our solar panel. To counter this, we use protective shielding. An example of such radiation is a van Allen belt.

·      Space dust- cosmic dust hits the spacecraft and turns into plasma.

·      Collisions- sometimes, interruptions in transmission data occurs and this can be dangerous because a collision could happen.

·      Incorrect orbit- Glitches in a rocket can cause a satellite to be in the wrong orbit.

·      Internal problems-

o   Reaction wheel- by adding or removing energy from a flywheel, torque is applied to a single axis of a spacecraft when wheel rotated (CW creates an internal torque on the craft going CW usually have a high pointing accuracy and can only rotate around center of mass)

o   Some of the pros of using this momentum wheel is that:

§  It does not rely on the magnetic field.

§  Storage of angular momentum

o   Some of the cons are:

§  It is a mechanism, so it requires lubrication and generates micro vibrations.

§  Saturation: the buildup of (enough) stored momentum to exceed the max speed of the wheel. (can be canceled using a magnetorquer)

o   IMUs can also be used in the system although they present the same problem of eventually requiring servicing.
